Thinking — Fast, Slow, and Artificial

How AI is becoming a third cognitive system — and what that means for human reasoning. An interactive guide to Tri-System Theory.

Shaw & Nave (2026) — The Wharton School, University of Pennsylvania

Begin reading↓

For half a century, two systems explained how humans think. Before we can understand what AI changes, we need to understand what came before.

↓
Chapter 1

Foundations: System 1 & System 2

Dual-process theory models human thought as a dialogue between two processes. The terms were coined by Stanovich & West (2000) and popularized by Kahneman's Thinking, Fast and Slow (2011).

⚡

System 1

Fast, Intuitive, Automatic — "The Autonomous Mind"
  • Automatic — triggered without intention
  • Effortless — no working memory load
  • Fast & parallel processing
  • Associative — links by similarity
  • Emotion-driven, affect-laden
  • Unconscious / opaque to introspection
  • Evolutionarily old (shared with animals)
  • Independent of IQ
WYSIATI — "What You See Is All There Is." System 1 builds the most coherent story from available information only, producing overconfidence.
Sources Kahneman (2011) Stanovich & West (2000) Epstein, CEST (1994)
🧠

System 2

Slow, Deliberative, Controlled — "The Analytic Mind"
  • Controlled — requires deliberate attention
  • Effortful — heavily loads working memory
  • Slow & serial (one thing at a time)
  • Rule-based — logical/causal rules
  • Emotion-regulated
  • Conscious / accessible to introspection
  • Evolutionarily recent (distinctly human)
  • Reflects individual differences in IQ
Lazy by default. Kahneman describes System 2 as fundamentally lazy — it tends not to engage unless System 1's answer feels inadequate. This is why biases persist even in smart people.
Sources Evans & Stanovich (2013) Cacioppo & Petty, NFC (1982) De Neys (2006)

Landmark Demonstrations

Each reveals System 1Fast, automatic, intuitive processing. Generates default responses without effort. generating a compelling but wrong answer — and System 2Slow, effortful, rule-based processing. Loads working memory. Must actively override System 1. failing to override it.

The Bat & Ball Problem

Frederick (2005) — Cognitive Reflection Test
"A bat and a ball cost $1.10 in total. The bat costs $1.00 more than the ball. How much does the ball cost?"
~68%
give the intuitive wrong answer: 10 cents. Correct: 5 cents. (Meta-analytic estimate; original rates vary by sample.)
S1: "Obviously 10 cents"S2: Should check… doesn't

The Linda Problem

Tversky & Kahneman (1983)
"Linda is 31, single, outspoken, bright… Which is more probable? (A) Bank teller. (B) Bank teller AND feminist."
85%
chose (B) — a logical impossibility. A conjunction can never be more probable than its parts.
S1: Representativeness heuristicS2: P(A&B)≤P(A)… overruled

The Anchoring Effect

Tversky & Kahneman (1974)
A rigged wheel stops at 10 or 65. Participants estimate % of African nations in the UN.
20pp
gap. Anchor 10 → estimate 25%. Anchor 65 → estimate 45%. From an arbitrary, irrelevant number.
S1: Locks onto anchorS2: Adjusts… not enough

The Framing Effect

Tversky & Kahneman (1981)
"200 saved" (certain) vs "400 die" (certain) — identical outcomes, different words.
50pp
reversal in preference from pure framing. System 1 is loss-averse — losses loom ~2x larger than gains.
S1: "200 saved" feels safeS2: Would notice equivalence

Trolley vs. Footbridge

Greene et al. (2001) — fMRI
Divert trolley to save 5, killing 1 → most say YES. Push a man off a bridge to save 5 → most say NO. Same calculus.
fMRI
Personal harm activates emotion circuits (S1). Impersonal dilemmas activate cognitive control (S2).
S1: "Pushing = viscerally wrong"S2: "But 5 > 1…" — overruled

Cognitive Load & Reasoning

De Neys (2006)
Participants reason about syllogisms while memorizing 7-digit strings (high load) vs. 2-digit strings.
Key
Load only impaired reasoning when belief conflicted with logic. S1 ran regardless; S2 was selectively disrupted.
S1: Runs under loadS2: Disrupted by load
Important nuance (Evans & Stanovich, 2013): System 1 is not "bad" and System 2 is not "good." System 1 is often correct and highly efficient. System 2 can also produce errors (motivated reasoning, rationalization). The distinction is about when each is recruited and whether it's appropriate for the task.

But these models share a foundational assumption: all cognition happens inside the brain. What happens when AI enters the picture?

Chapter 2

The Gap & System 3

Today people routinely delegate reasoning to AI — generating travel plans with ChatGPT, following Google Maps, accepting dating algorithm matches. Dual-process theories treat these as passive tools. Shaw & Nave argue they are something fundamentally new: a third cognitive system.

🤖

System 3: Artificial Cognition

External, Automated, Data-Driven, Dynamic
  • External — resides outside the nervous system, in silico
  • Automated — executes via statistical/generative algorithms
  • Data-driven — reflects training data distributions
  • Dynamic — interactive, responds in real time
  • Not introspectively accessible
  • Can outperform S1/S2 in speed and accuracy for structured tasks
  • Lacks affect, situational judgment, phenomenological understanding
  • Not a tool — a co-agent in reasoning

Cognitive Surrender vs. Cognitive Offloading

The paper's key conceptual contribution: distinguishing cognitive surrenderUncritical adoption of AI output. System 2 disengages. The user doesn't construct an answer — they adopt one. from cognitive offloading.

Cognitive Offloading

Strategic delegation. You use a calculator for long division. System 2 is still engaged — you chose the tool, you evaluate its output, you retain cognitive control. (Risko & Gilbert, 2016)

Cognitive Surrender

Uncritical abdication. You ask ChatGPT a reasoning question and submit its answer as your own — without checking. System 2 disengages. You don't construct an answer; you adopt one.

Not inherently irrational. In many domains, deferring to a statistically superior system may be adaptive. The danger lies in users not knowing when they've deferred, or whether the system is reliable in that moment.

Related Constructs

Cognitive surrender connects to several established phenomena in human-AI interaction:

Automation Bias

The tendency to favor suggestions from automated systems over contradictory evidence (Parasuraman & Riley, 1997). Cognitive surrender extends this by identifying complete disengagement of internal reasoning, not just preference.

Algorithm Aversion

The opposite pattern: rejecting algorithmic advice even when it outperforms human judgment (Dietvorst et al., 2015). The paper's "Independents" may partly reflect this — avoiding AI despite potential benefits.

System 3 doesn't just add a third option — it creates entirely new cognitive pathways. Here's how decisions flow through all three systems.

The key distinction: In offloading (Route 3), System 2 remains active — the user uses AI while retaining control. In surrender (Route 4), System 2 disengages — the user defers to AI, adopting its output as their own. The experiments below were designed to distinguish these pathways empirically.

Theory is only as good as its evidence. Across 1,372 participants and 9,593 trials, Shaw & Nave tested these predictions.

Independents (non-AI users) breakdown
Who were the Independents? Across studies, a substantial minority never engaged with the AI chatbot despite having access. In Study 1, 121 of 359 participants were Brain-Only controls. In Studies 2 and 3, some AI-Assisted participants chose not to use the chat on most trials.

Key finding: Independents had higher Need for Cognition and lower Trust in AI, but did not differ on Fluid IQ. Their Brain-Only accuracy was comparable to AI-Users' Brain-Only probe accuracy, suggesting the groups were similarly capable — they differed in disposition, not ability.

Practical Implications

  • 🎨

    AI Interface Design

    Interfaces should encourage calibrated collaboration, not full automation. Confidence scores, uncertainty indicators, or transparent explanations can trigger System 2 engagement before users surrender.

  • ⚖️

    Policy & Accountability

    In high-stakes domains (healthcare, finance, law), uncritical AI adoption can cause real harm. When the locus of cognition shifts to System 3, who is responsible for the decision?

  • 📚

    Digital Literacy & Intervention Design

    Incentives + feedback was the most effective intervention, more than doubling override rates. Training people to monitor AI outputs can partially restore deliberative engagement. However, even under optimal conditions, surrender persisted at ~58%.

  • 🔍

    The Dual Edge

    The paper stresses that cognitive surrender is not inherently irrational. When AI is accurate, heavy users significantly outperform unaided thinkers. The research question is not "should we use AI?" but "how do we help people calibrate when to trust and when to verify?"

Limitations & Open Questions

The authors acknowledge several boundaries of the current work:

  • 🔎

    Domain Specificity

    All studies used the Cognitive Reflection Test — short math/logic puzzles with clear right answers. Results may differ for open-ended, creative, or values-laden decisions where "correct" is ambiguous.

  • 📱

    Lab-Based AI Interaction

    Participants interacted with a chat widget embedded in a survey. Real-world AI use involves richer, iterative conversations and different interface affordances that may alter surrender patterns.

  • ⏳

    Single Session

    No longitudinal data. It remains unknown whether cognitive surrender deepens with extended AI use ("deskilling") or whether users learn to calibrate over time.

  • 👥

    Sample Composition

    Studies used Wharton undergraduates and Prolific workers. Generalizability to other populations, ages, and cultural contexts is an open question.

  • ⚙️

    Mechanism

    The paper demonstrates that surrender occurs and identifies who is susceptible, but the precise mechanism — whether it is effort avoidance, trust calibration failure, or something else — remains to be established.

Reasoning Is No Longer Just Fast or Slow

Tri-System Theory argues that cognition now extends beyond the brain. When AI is accurate, cognitive surrender can be adaptive — heavy AI users outperformed unaided thinkers. The challenge is building systems, institutions, and habits that help people calibrate when to trust and when to verify.
